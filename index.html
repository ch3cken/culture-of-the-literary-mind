What is “Culture of the Literary Mind in 20th Century America”?

"Culture of the Literary Mind in 20th Century America" is a Digital Humanities project developed by Enrique Jose Delgado Garcia, for the 2026 Spring course "Introduction to Digital Humanities" taught by professor Donghyun Woo. The project aims to observe and give insight into American culture through the lens of popular authors at the time.

This webpage provides a visualization timeline from the 1890s to the 2000s about the subjects of books that best-selling authors wrote about in the form of a word cloud. Each decade has its own word cloud, where bigger words were more present in more authors' literature compared to the smaller words/phrases in the cloud. For precise numbers, one can click the decade dot on the timeline to see a list of subjects, along with their "frequency" (the number of best-selling authors that wrote about such subjects). Finally, if one would like to see the trends of individual subjects, one can click on the subject in the word cloud or in the "subjects by frequency" list to see a timeline of that specific subject's frequency in the 20th century (including 1890s and 2000s).

What did I do for this project?

It's of great importance for me to express my methodology exactly, so that no one is deceived by what I mean by "best-selling author" and to understand where these "subjects" come from in the first place.

"Best-selling authors" were found by looking at the authors of Publishers' Weekly's list of best-selling novels in United States in each year, from 1890 to 2009. These authors were then considered to be the important authors to American culture, given that they had at least one best-selling book in that decade. To be frank, I had first planned to do this project based only on the subjects of best-selling books only, rather than their authors. However, due to limitations of some books not having much readily-accessible data on them, and books having multiple editions and versions, and those having different data on them, I decided to focus on those books' authors, given that they likely were the main "influencers" in the literary American world, and what they wrote about would give a sufficient glance into American culture. To be sure, this is a limitation in my work, alongside the fact that I'm only included novel writers, as opposed to general book writers.

The subjects that the authors wrote about were then found using the OpenLibrary Search API. With a python program, I was able to extract the subjects that each author wrote about in that respective decade, since many of the books in the OpenLibrary API have a field "subjects" which represents the topics of the books. The program went through all the books written in that decade by that author and accumulate all the subjects that the author wrote about. This is where another limitation in my work comes into play: OpenLibrary API's openness. People are free to submit subjects of books which had many implications in this work. It meant that older authors naturally had less subjects attached to them, and also some of the topics were nonsensical, repetitive, or were in other languages. I'd argue that this makes my research "reader-centric" as the subjects extracted were subjects that readers believed were applicable to each work, and the fact that any registered reader could submit subjects allows for multiplicity of reader opinion; not one narrative is chosen, but multiple narrative topics are allowed to be given. However, I understand if that's not a strong defense for this work. Because of some of the subjects' nonsensical and repetitive nature, I had to clean up many of their strings, such as making a topic like "London (England)" into two different topics "London" and "England". I also separated topics that appeared together such as "FICTION / Historical" becoming "FICTION" and "Historical".

To see the precise method, one can go to the GitHub page for this project. I have placed all program and data there, along with a README to understand it.
